*Notebook / private markets*

Clarity is often framed as an information problem.

In Canadian private markets, it’s rarely that.

It’s an incentive problem.

There is no shortage of capital in Canada. Large institutions, pension funds, private vehicles—capital is available across the spectrum.

But it doesn’t move evenly.

It concentrates.

Capital tends to flow where:

- outcomes are predictable
- decisions are defensible
- mistakes are survivable

Not necessarily where returns are highest.

This creates a structural imbalance:

- large, visible deals get overcapitalized
- smaller, less intermediated opportunities remain underpriced or ignored
- “safe” assets get bid up beyond their actual risk profile

The result is not a lack of capital.

It’s a misalignment between:

- where capital *can* go
- and where decision-makers are *willing* to allocate it

In practice, this shows up quietly.

Processes that favor consensus over conviction. Structures designed to minimize career risk rather than maximize returns. A bias toward scale, even when scale dilutes edge.

Canada doesn’t underinvest.

It overprotects.

And over time, that protection becomes its own form of risk.
